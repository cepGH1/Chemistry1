Covalent Bonding

Outer shell electrons are shared by the atoms bonded together. Each atom 'sees' that there are enough electrons in it's outer shell to make that shell appear to be full.

Covalent bonding happens when non-metals bond to each other

Covalent bonding can lead to simple molecules, with just a few atoms in them, or to large covalent lattices of infinite size.

Diamonds, plastics, graphite, glass and quartz are all covalently bonded.

Dot Cross Diagrams for some simple Covalent Molecules

The sharing of electrons can be shown using dot cross diagrams. The electrons are shown as dots and crosses. the shared ones appear in the overlap of the circles that represent the outer electron shell of the atoms.

Simple covalent molecules have low boiling points because there are not many forces between the molecules, just weak van der Waals forces

The hydrogen atom

A Hydrogen atom has a single electron in it's outer shell

Hydrogen gas

Hydrogen usually exists as a diatomic molecule with a single covalent bond between the atoms. Each atom shares it's electron. So both atoms appear to have a full outer electronic shell

Giant Covalent Lattices

Diamond and silicon dioxide are probably the best known examples of structures that use covalent bonding to form a neverending framework between their atoms. These compounds have high melting points because it is difficult to break the atoms apart. If you break one of the bonds then there are still 3 more in place and that bond might well reform before you break the next.
